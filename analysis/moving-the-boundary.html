Who Moves the Boundary?

The proof boundary exists as a fact. Moving it is a human choice. But who exactly is the human making that choice?

Ethical Conceptual Historical Psychological Cross-cutting

Three Competing Intuitions

In the conversation, Keith and Matt circle around three different models of who moves the proof boundary:

Model 1: The Inventor Moves It

Keith's initial intuition: the person who creates the technology moves the boundary. Self-driving cars exist — therefore the boundary has moved.

Matt's correction: "any inventor can come up with any shit." The existence of a technology doesn't move the boundary. The decision to deploy it does.

Model 2: The Deployer Moves It

Matt's position: the hospital administrator who decides to purchase and install the Therac-25 is the one who moves the boundary. They're "the last decision maker to have influence over taking the human out of it."

Keith: "so i think then you're saying that it wasn't the company that built the machine that moved the proof boundary it was the hospital administrator"

Matt: "they're the last decision maker to have influence over taking the human out of it"

Model 3: The Chain of Decisions

Reality is messier: it's a chain — inventor, manufacturer, regulator, insurer, purchaser, operator. Each makes a choice that enables or constrains the next.

But Matt identifies the critical decision point: the moment a human is removed from the loop. This is the definitive move.

The Chair Test

The Chair Test

Keith asks: every time you sit in a chair without checking if it'll hold you, isn't that trusting a system? Isn't that the proof boundary?

Matt says no: "there's no decision — you're not entrusting your chair to make a decision."

This reveals a crucial constraint in the definition: the proof boundary is specifically about delegating decisions, not just trusting physical objects. A chair doesn't decide anything. An elevator does (which floor, when to open doors). A radiation machine does (how much dose to deliver).

Temporal and Local Nature

Matt emphasizes the proof boundary is temporal and local:

  • "state by state, municipality by municipality, everyone puts their own proof boundary"
  • "every time a different hospital administrator of a different hospital decides to start using or stop using this machine they're moving the proof boundary"
  • When bad news about Therac-25 came out and everyone mothballed them — that was also moving the proof boundary (back)

This means the proof boundary is not a single global line. It's millions of individual decisions, made continuously, at every level of society.

Psychological Dimension: Why Keith Resists

Keith keeps pushing back because his practical experience tells him:

  1. Most people don't know or care about proofs when they adopt technology
  2. The drunk hotel manager story illustrates this — decisions are often arbitrary, emotional, financial
  3. This feels like it undermines the importance of proofs

But Matt's response is subtle: he's not saying people should ignore proofs. He's saying the proof boundary moves whether or not anyone does the proofs. The moral claim is separate from the descriptive one.

Conceptual Dimension: Existence vs Movement

Matt makes a distinction Keith initially misses:

The proof boundary exists as a descriptive fact (where is the line between human judgment and machine automation right now?).

Moving the proof boundary is a human choice (deciding to automate something, or deciding to de-automate it).

Doing proofs at the boundary is a separate choice (once you've moved it, do you verify the machine behaves correctly?).

Concept Type Question Who Answers
Proof boundary (exists) Descriptive Where is the line? Observable fact
Moving the boundary Decision Should we automate this? Decision-maker (deployer)
Doing the proofs Action Should we verify the automation? Engineers, regulators
Proof boundary consequences Normative What happens if we don't verify? Reality (catastrophes)

Historical Dimension: Accountability Through History

From the book's content on civic struggles and human rights:

  • The question of "who decides" echoes throughout history
  • Apartheid moved proof boundaries by delegating racial classification to bureaucratic systems
  • The civil rights movement was partly about who gets to move these boundaries
  • Indigenous rights movements challenged systems that automated dispossession

The proof boundary concept scales from software to society.

Ethical Dimension: The Insurance Problem

Matt makes a devastating observation about how hospital administrators actually think:

Matt: "if you as an administrator you have that insurance then even if this machine kills a patient your shareholders are not losing their shirts no one's going broke over this they only died that's how hospital administrators think"

This reveals a dark truth about moving the proof boundary in practice: the person making the decision often doesn't bear the consequences. The cost structure is misaligned:

  • Administrator bears: financial risk (mitigated by insurance)
  • Patient bears: physical risk (unmitigable)

This misalignment is why Matt argues for mandatory proofs — to close the gap between who decides and who suffers.

Cross-Cutting: Keith's Elevator Operator Story

Keith's story about the drunk, indebted hotel manager firing the elevator operator is actually a perfect illustration of Matt's point, even though Keith thinks it contradicts it:

  1. The manager moves the proof boundary (fires human, enables automation) ✓
  2. The decision is based on arbitrary personal circumstances, not proofs ✓
  3. If the automated elevator kills someone, the manager is responsible ✓
  4. If the elevator had been formally verified, the risk would be lower ✓

Keith and Matt agree on all of this. The confusion is that Keith thinks his story shows proofs are irrelevant to the proof boundary, while Matt sees it as showing why proofs are urgently needed when the boundary moves.

The Real Agreement

Keith and Matt aren't actually disagreeing about the facts. They're disagreeing about the emphasis. Keith says: "Look, people move the boundary for dumb reasons all the time — so proofs can't be central to the concept." Matt says: "Exactly — that's the whole problem. That's why proofs must be mandated."
