Science, Math, and LLMs: Three Epistemic Worlds

Matt draws sharp lines between scientific knowledge, mathematical knowledge, and LLM outputs. These distinctions are foundational to understanding why the proof boundary matters.

Mathematical Conceptual Computer Science Cross-cutting

Three Ways of Knowing

Matt spends considerable time in the conversation distinguishing between three fundamentally different epistemic domains. Each has its own basis for truth, its own method of verification, and its own failure modes. Understanding these distinctions is essential to understanding what the proof boundary is — and what it isn't.

The critical column is Verification. Science has experiments. Mathematics has proof checkers. LLM outputs have... nothing inherent. They produce text that looks like knowledge but has no built-in mechanism for confirming its truth. This is not a temporary limitation that better training will fix; it is a structural feature of how statistical language models work.

Matt's Physics Detour

Matt spends significant time on a physics analogy about the event horizon. Two types of "can't see" in the universe:

  1. Too faint to detect — a practical limitation. Better telescopes, more sensitive instruments, longer observation times might solve it. The star is there; we just can't see it yet.
  2. Beyond the event horizon — a theoretical limitation. No instrument of any kind, no matter how advanced, can retrieve information from beyond an event horizon. It is not a matter of engineering. It is a consequence of the laws of physics.

This maps to the proof boundary:

  1. Things we haven't proven yet — practical limitation. More effort, better tools, more time could produce a proof. The proof exists in principle; we just haven't found it.
  2. Things that can't be proven — theoretical limitation. Gödel's incompleteness theorems, Turing's halting problem, Rice's theorem. There are true statements that no finite proof can establish, programs whose behavior no algorithm can predict, properties that no analysis can determine.

The proof boundary lives between these — it's about the practical choices we make within the theoretical limits. We can't prove everything (Gödel forbids it), but we can prove far more than we currently do (most software has zero formal verification). The boundary is not at the edge of what's theoretically possible; it's at the edge of what we've chosen to bother with.

The LLM Problem

Matt: “The better an LLM is at actually understanding science, the less likely it is to come up with anything original... and if it does come up with something original, it's probably a hallucination.”

This is because:

  1. Good scientific reasoning recognizes the limits of what can be known. A well-trained model that truly understands physics will know that certain questions require experiments, not arguments.
  2. LLMs trained on good science will reflect those limits. They will reproduce the caution, the hedging, the "we don't know yet" that characterizes genuine scientific writing.
  3. Going beyond those limits requires new evidence, not new reasoning. Science advances by discovering facts about the world, not by cleverer manipulation of existing facts.
  4. LLMs can only recombine existing reasoning — they can't run experiments. They can hypothesize, but they cannot test. They can predict, but they cannot measure.

This creates a paradox for LLMs in science: the better they are, the more they will defer to the boundaries of existing knowledge. And when they go beyond those boundaries, they are most likely hallucinating — producing plausible-sounding text that has no grounding in reality.

The External World

Matt connects this to Richard Sutton's concept of an "external world":

  • Agentic systems need something outside themselves to verify against
  • In science: physical experiments
  • In math: proof checkers (Lean, Coq)
  • In games: rule enforcers (AlphaGo's legal-moves checker)
  • In software: formal verification tools (TLA+)

Without an external verifier, the system is talking to itself. Matt calls this the fundamental limitation of basic agentic systems. An LLM generating text and then evaluating its own text is like a student grading their own exam — the process produces confidence, not correctness.

The external world provides what the system itself cannot: an independent check on truth. This is why AlphaGo works so well — it has a perfect external verifier (the rules of Go). It is why AlphaProof can generate valid mathematics — Lean rejects every invalid proof step. And it is why ungrounded LLM outputs are unreliable — there is nothing outside the model to say "no."

The Proof Boundary as Interface

The proof boundary is the interface between the system and its external verifier. It's where machine reasoning meets something that can say “no, that's wrong.” Without this interface, automation is unverified delegation — trust without evidence.

Keith's Question: “Isn't Pure Math Verifiable?”

Keith asks this showing genuine curiosity. Matt's answer reveals the key distinction:

Matt: “It's verifiable by its own assumptions but there's nothing you would measure in the real world to confirm that math is correct... you can derive π mathematically, you can't derive the speed of light mathematically.”

Math is self-contained. Its truths are consequences of axioms. You don't need the universe to confirm that 2 + 2 = 4 or that π is irrational. The proof is the territory.

Science requires the universe. No amount of mathematical reasoning can tell you the speed of light. You have to go measure it. The map must match the territory.

LLMs require... what?

The answer: LLMs require the same external verifiers that science and math do. They can draft a proof (like math) or suggest an experiment (like science), but they can't verify either one. That's why the proof boundary matters — it's about where verification happens.

An LLM can write a Lean proof, but Lean must check it. An LLM can propose a hypothesis, but a lab must test it. An LLM can generate code, but a type checker must validate it. In every case, the LLM is on one side of the proof boundary, and the verifier is on the other. The boundary is the point where "this sounds right" becomes "this is right."

Connections to the Book

The book covers several case studies that illustrate the external-verifier principle in action:

  • AlphaGo/AlphaZero: AI that does have an external verifier (legal-moves checker) → this is why it works. The game rules provide a perfect, complete, deterministic external world. Every move is either legal or illegal. Every game is either won or lost.
  • HumanEval: Code benchmarks with external test suites → this is how LLM code is checked. The tests provide a partial external verifier. But tests only check what they test — they cannot guarantee correctness beyond their own scope.
  • AlphaProof: AI generating proofs verified by Lean → this is the proof boundary in action. The LLM proposes; Lean disposes. The proof checker is the external world that makes the output trustworthy.
  • DIN-SQL: Natural language → SQL with executable verification → domain-specific proof boundary. The database itself serves as the external verifier: either the query returns correct results or it doesn't.

Each of these is an example of moving the proof boundary with appropriate verification. They work because someone ensured that the automated system has an external world to check itself against. They represent Matt's thesis in practice: you can automate, but you must verify.

Cross-Cutting: Why Keith Gets Lost in the Physics Detour

At one point Keith says:

Keith: “Bring this back towards your general thesis — I'm getting a little bit lost.”

Matt's physics tangent is actually foundational to his argument, but Keith can't see the connection because:

  1. Keith thinks concretely (software, business decisions, client expectations). He needs the specific before the general.
  2. Matt thinks in principles (epistemic limits, verification domains, theoretical boundaries). He builds from the general to the specific.
  3. The physics example is an analogy for the principle, but Keith needs the principle stated directly before the analogy can land.

This communication pattern — Matt explaining the principle through analogy while Keith needs the principle stated directly — recurs throughout the conversation. Matt says "event horizon" and means "there are hard limits on what can be known." Keith hears "event horizon" and thinks "why are we talking about black holes?"

The pattern is not a failure of intelligence on either side. It is a difference in epistemic style. Keith is an empiricist: show me the data, show me the case, show me the code. Matt is a rationalist: understand the principle, and the cases follow. Both approaches are valid. The friction arises because neither recognizes, in the moment, that the other is operating from a different starting point.

For Keith to understand Matt's physics detour, he would need Matt to say: "I'm about to explain a physics concept, and here's why it matters to software: there are some things that are hard to verify (we need better tools) and some things that are impossible to verify (no tool will ever work). The proof boundary is about the first category, not the second." With that framing, the event horizon analogy becomes illuminating rather than disorienting.
