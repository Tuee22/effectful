Untangling the Proof Boundary

An analysis of the March 3, 2026 conversation between Keith Seim and Matt Nowak, identifying key tensions, misunderstandings, and deep questions about the nature of the proof boundary.

Context

This conversation is one in a series between Keith (a software development leader exploring formal methods) and Matt Nowak (author of The Proof Boundary: Where Verification and Judgment Meet). Keith is trying to understand Matt's central concept — the "proof boundary" — and keeps running into a specific conceptual knot: the definition of the term doesn't seem to require proofs, yet the entire thesis is about why proofs matter. This analysis maps every point of friction and explores each from multiple angles.

Source material: 1h 15m Zoom conversation, WhatsApp thread, Keith's written feedback on the paper, and the book manuscript.

The Central Tension

Matt defines the "proof boundary" without mentioning proofs, then spends the entire paper arguing proofs are essential. Keith can't reconcile the definition with the argument.

Key Questions Identified

  1. Why is it called the "proof boundary" if the definition doesn't mention proofs?
  2. What's the difference between a valid proof and a sound proof, and why does Keith keep forgetting?
  3. Who actually moves the proof boundary — the inventor or the deployer?
  4. How does the Therac-25 case illuminate the concept?
  5. Is it ever acceptable to move the boundary without doing the proofs?
  6. How do science, math, and LLM outputs differ epistemically?

Matt's Layered Argument

What Keith is missing is that Matt is making a three-layer argument:

  • Layer 1 (Definitional): The proof boundary is the line between human judgment and machine automation. It's a descriptive concept — it just is.
  • Layer 2 (Technical): When you move this line (automate something), formal proofs become the only reliable way to ensure safety. This is a technical claim about the nature of software.
  • Layer 3 (Moral): Choosing to move the boundary without doing the proofs is a moral error with potentially catastrophic consequences.

Keith keeps trying to find proofs IN Layer 1, when they live in Layers 2 and 3.
