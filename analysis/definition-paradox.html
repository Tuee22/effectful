The Definition Paradox

Why does "proof boundary" not mention proofs in its definition? Unpacking the central conceptual knot.

Conceptual Linguistic Psychological Mathematical

The Paradox Stated

Matt defines the proof boundary as: "the limit between decisions that must be subjectively judged by a human and those which humans trust machines to make in an automated fashion."

Keith immediately notices: "you didn't say the word proof." Matt agrees and says he could have called it "pineapple upside down cake" and everything would still apply.

This creates a paradox: if the name is arbitrary, why use a name that implies mathematical proofs are central? And if proofs are central, why aren't they in the definition?

Keith: In that definition you just gave me, it has nothing to do with proofs. You didn't say the word proof. You said it's the boundary between humans making decisions and machines making decisions.

Matt: Right.

Keith: So then why is it called the proof boundary?

Matt: I could have called it pineapple upside down cake. It wouldn't matter. The concept would be the same. The word "proof" is in the name because proofs are the mechanism — the thing you should be doing when you move that line.

The Knot

Keith hears this exchange as confirmation that proofs are irrelevant to the concept. Matt means it as confirmation that the definition describes the phenomenon, while the name points to the prescription. They are talking past each other about the relationship between naming, defining, and arguing.

The Resolution: Name vs Definition vs Thesis

The confusion dissolves once you separate three distinct layers that Matt has compressed into a single term.

The Name

"Proof boundary" is a label. Matt explicitly states it's arbitrary. He chose it because proofs are the mechanism by which safety is achieved when the boundary moves — but the boundary's existence doesn't depend on proofs.

Analogy: "The speed of light" doesn't define light in terms of speed. Light exists independently of its speed being measured. Similarly, the proof boundary exists independently of whether anyone does a proof.

The Definition

The definition describes a phenomenon: where humans draw the line between their judgment and machine autonomy. This is a sociological and organizational fact, not a mathematical one. Every organization that uses software has a proof boundary, whether or not they have ever heard the term or done a formal proof.

The Thesis

The thesis is that when you move this line without doing proofs, bad things happen. The thesis connects the phenomenon (defined without proofs) to the mechanism (proofs) via consequences (catastrophes).

Definition (no proofs) ──────► Phenomenon exists The line between human judgment and machine autonomy is real. Thesis (requires proofs) ────► Proofs needed when phenomenon changes Moving the line without verification produces catastrophic consequences. Name ("proof boundary") ─────► Labels the phenomenon by its thesis The name encodes the prescription, not the description.
The Three-Layer Structure

The definition tells you what exists. The thesis tells you what to do about it. The name tells you what the thesis recommends. Keith is looking for the thesis inside the definition, and finding it absent, concludes the whole framework is incoherent. But the definition is deliberately proof-free — that's what makes it an objective claim about the world rather than an argument.

Psychological Dimension

Why Keith Gets Stuck

Keith is a practical software developer. He thinks in terms of concrete mappings: "word X means thing Y." When "proof boundary" doesn't map to "boundary involving proofs," his mental model breaks.

This is related to the linguistic concept of compositionality — the assumption that the meaning of a compound expression is determined by the meanings of its parts. "Proof" + "boundary" should mean "boundary of proofs." But it doesn't. It means "boundary where proofs should be done." The preposition shifts from of to where, and that small grammatical pivot carries the entire conceptual weight.

Keith's instinct is not wrong as a general heuristic. Most technical terms are compositional: a "hash table" is a table that uses hashing, a "binary tree" is a tree that branches in twos. "Proof boundary" breaks this pattern, and Keith's pattern-matching correctly flags the anomaly. The problem is that he interprets the anomaly as incoherence rather than as a different kind of naming convention.

Why Matt Gets Frustrated

Matt has internalized the three-layer structure so deeply that the connection feels obvious. When Keith says "it has nothing to do with proofs," Matt hears an attack on his thesis, not a genuine question about the definition.

Matt: I've truly failed here to express myself clearly. Except to me.

This is a moment of striking self-awareness. Matt recognizes the communication gap but frames it as his own failure of expression rather than a structural difficulty inherent in the concept. Both framings have merit: the concept is hard to communicate, and the naming choice does make it harder.

Conceptual Dimension

Objective vs Normative Claims

Matt makes an important distinction (that Keith asks to define): the proof boundary is an objective claim about what is (where the line exists), not a normative claim about what should be.

But Matt's paper is largely normative — arguing for what should happen when the boundary moves. The definition is descriptive, the thesis is prescriptive. This two-level structure trips Keith up because most concepts he encounters in software development are either purely descriptive ("a REST API is...") or purely prescriptive ("you should use dependency injection because..."). Matt's concept is both, with the descriptive part carrying a prescriptive name.

The Category Error

Keith tries three "buckets" to categorize why the boundary moves:

  1. Arbitrary human decision — the drunk hotel manager fires the elevator operator
  2. Perceived reliability — a salesperson convinces a buyer the software is safe enough
  3. Objective verification — formal proofs demonstrate correctness

Matt says these aren't three different things — they're all instances of moving the proof boundary, with different motivations. Keith is categorizing by cause; Matt is categorizing by effect.

Keith's View (by cause) Matt's View (by effect)
Three separate categories with different epistemic status One phenomenon with multiple triggers
Only bucket 3 involves proofs All three should involve proofs
The buckets explain why the boundary moves The thesis explains what should happen when it does

Neither view is wrong. They operate at different levels of analysis. But as long as Keith is sorting by cause and Matt is sorting by effect, they will continue to talk past each other.

Mathematical Dimension

What "Proof" Actually Means Here

In formal mathematics, a proof is a sequence of logical steps from axioms to a conclusion, verifiable by a mechanical checker. The proof boundary's connection to this formal notion is specific but indirect:

When humans delegate to machines (move the boundary), the decisions the machine makes can potentially be formally verified — this is where it's "possible to do a proof" (Matt's words). Whether anyone bothers is a separate question. The proof boundary marks the zone where formal verification becomes both possible and (according to Matt) obligatory.

This is not the only meaning of "proof" in play. Matt's usage draws on several traditions simultaneously, which contributes to the confusion.

Definition: Proof (mathematical)

A finite sequence of logical deductions from axioms and previously established theorems that establishes the truth of a statement with certainty. In formal verification, this is mechanized: a proof assistant (Coq, Lean, Isabelle) checks every step.

Definition: Proof Boundary (Matt's definition)

The limit between decisions that must be subjectively judged by a human and those which humans trust machines to make in an automated fashion. Note: this definition contains no reference to proofs, verification, or formal methods. It is purely descriptive.

Definition: Proof Obligation (the normative claim)

The thesis that when the proof boundary moves — when a decision previously made by humans is delegated to a machine — a formal proof of the machine's correctness is the only reliable way to ensure safety. This is the prescriptive claim that gives the concept its name but is deliberately excluded from its definition.

Historical Dimension

The name "proof boundary" evokes a long tradition of using "proof" in different senses:

  • Burden of proof (legal) — who bears the obligation to demonstrate a claim? The party moving the boundary bears the burden of proving the machine is safe.
  • Mathematical proof (logic) — a chain of reasoning that establishes truth with certainty. The gold standard that Matt argues should be applied at the boundary.
  • Proof of concept (engineering) — a demonstration that something works. Weaker than formal proof, but often what practitioners settle for.

Matt's usage is closest to burden of proof — who bears the responsibility to verify? The legal metaphor is apt: when you automate a decision, you take on the burden of proving the automation is correct. The mathematical meaning provides the method for meeting that burden. The engineering meaning is what most people actually do, and what Matt argues is insufficient.

Cross-Cutting Concern: The Naming Problem

Why names matter: if Matt had called it the "delegation boundary" or the "automation boundary," Keith would not have been confused about the definition. The word "proof" creates an expectation of mathematical content in the definition itself.

But "delegation boundary" loses the normative force — it doesn't carry the implication that verification is needed. The name is the thesis compressed into two words. This is both its strength (memorable, evocative, argues for itself) and its weakness (misleading about the definition, creates false expectations about what the concept is versus what it demands).

Alternative Name What It Captures What It Loses
Delegation boundary The definition (human-to-machine handoff) The thesis (proofs are required)
Automation boundary The phenomenon (what gets automated) The moral weight (consequences of failure)
Verification boundary The prescription (verify before automating) The specificity (formal proof, not just testing)
Proof boundary The thesis + the mechanism The definition (sounds like proofs define it)

There is no perfect name. Every option trades clarity on one layer for confusion on another. Matt chose the name that best communicates his thesis at the expense of his definition. For a polemic work, this is arguably the right choice. For a pedagogical one, it creates exactly the friction Keith experiences.
